# Chief Revenue Officer Skills

This skill captures foundational and advanced knowledge for a Chief Revenue Officer (CRO) at a B2B SaaS / AI-native startup. It covers strategy, frameworks, sales methodology, RevOps, partner ecosystems, customer success, metrics, team leadership, and the modern revenue tech stack.

---

## Role Context: CRO at a B2B SaaS / AI Startup

A CRO owns the entire revenue engine — from first marketing touch through closed-won to expansion and renewal. The role emerged in SaaS to eliminate the silos between Marketing, Sales, Customer Success, and Partnerships that cause revenue leakage and misaligned incentives.

A CRO owns:
- **Revenue strategy** — unified plan for top-line growth across all channels
- **Sales leadership** — recruiting, managing, and coaching the sales organization
- **Go-to-market execution** — ICP definition, pricing strategy, channel mix, launch sequencing
- **Revenue Operations (RevOps)** — the systems, processes, and data infrastructure of the revenue engine
- **Partner & channel development** — ecosystem, reseller, referral, and technology partnerships
- **Customer success alignment** — renewals, expansion revenue, NRR, and churn prevention
- **Forecasting & pipeline management** — predictable, data-driven revenue prediction
- **Cross-functional alignment** — marketing, product, CS, and finance all rowing to one plan

### Core Responsibilities

- Define and own the company's revenue strategy aligned to growth milestones and investor expectations
- Build, hire, and manage a world-class sales team (AEs, SDRs, SEs, CS)
- Design and refine the go-to-market motion: ICP, positioning, pricing, channels
- Lead revenue forecasting at weekly, monthly, and quarterly cadences
- Manage the full revenue tech stack (CRM, sales engagement, BI, revenue intelligence)
- Drive RevOps to ensure clean data, automated processes, and one source of truth
- Build and scale the partner and channel ecosystem (referral, reseller, technology)
- Own the customer success function (or partner tightly with the CCO) to maximize NRR
- Report revenue performance to the CEO and board (ARR, pipeline, win rates, NRR)
- Align Marketing (MQLs/pipeline creation), Sales (pipeline conversion), and CS (retention/expansion)
- Enforce and iterate on the sales playbook: discovery, qualification, demo, proposal, close
- Set quota, compensation plans, and territories for the sales organization
- Lead pricing strategy and packaging in collaboration with Product and Finance

## Core Frameworks

### 1. Revenue Operations (RevOps) Framework

RevOps is the operational backbone of the CRO's organization. It unifies marketing, sales, and customer success under shared data, shared processes, and shared accountability.

**The RevOps Pillars:**

| Pillar | Description |
|---|---|
| People | Align roles, responsibilities, and incentives across revenue-generating functions |
| Process | Standardize lead-to-cash workflows, handoff SLAs, and lifecycle stage definitions |
| Technology | CRM as single source of truth; integrate all tools cleanly |
| Data | Shared definitions (MQL, SQL, opportunity, customer), clean CRM data, unified reporting |

**RevOps Operating Rhythm:**
- **Daily:** Pipeline hygiene checks, rep activity dashboards, deal risk alerts
- **Weekly:** Pipeline review, forecast call, SDR performance vs. activity targets
- **Monthly:** Funnel conversion analysis, RevOps metric review, comp plan vs. attainment
- **Quarterly:** GTM strategy review, territory and quota planning, tooling audit

**RevOps Lifecycle Stages:**
```
Lead → MQL → SAL (Sales Accepted Lead) → SQL → Opportunity → Proposal → Closed Won/Lost → Customer → Renewal/Expansion
```
Each stage must have: clear entry/exit criteria, owner, SLA, and conversion metric.

### 2. Go-to-Market (GTM) Strategy Framework

#### ICP Definition (Ideal Customer Profile)
Build your ICP on three dimensions:
1. **Firmographic:** Company size (employees, ARR), industry, geography, funding stage
2. **Technographic:** Current tech stack, tools they already use, integration compatibility
3. **Behavioral:** Buying signals (job postings, product usage, engagement history), pain indicators

**ICP Template:**
- Industry: [e.g., B2B SaaS, FinTech]
- Company size: [e.g., 50–500 employees]
- Revenue/stage: [e.g., Series A–C]
- Buyer personas: [e.g., VP of Engineering, Head of Operations]
- Pain: [e.g., manual workflows, lack of automation]
- Trigger events: [e.g., recent funding, new CTO hire, headcount growth]

#### GTM Motion Selection
| Motion | Best For | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Product-Led Growth (PLG) | Self-serve, low ACV, developer tools | Free tier, in-product upgrade, viral loops |
| Sales-Led Growth (SLG) | Enterprise, high ACV, complex buying committee | SDR/AE motion, multi-stakeholder deals |
| Marketing-Led Growth (MLG) | Mid-market, strong content/brand | Inbound-heavy, high MQL volume |
| Partner-Led Growth | Ecosystem plays, indirect channels | Resellers, SIs, ISV integrations |
| Hybrid (PLG + SLG) | The dominant SaaS model at scale | PQL handoff: self-serve → sales assist |

#### Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore)
- Early adopters (innovators) buy on vision; cross the "chasm" to the early majority by solving a specific, concrete pain point for a beachhead segment
- **Beachhead strategy:** Dominate one narrow ICP first, then expand — don't spread thin across segments
- Use reference customers to build credibility for the next segment

### 3. Sales Pipeline Management

#### Sales Stages (Standard B2B SaaS)
| Stage | Definition | Key Activities | Exit Criteria |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prospecting | Identified target account | Research, ICP qualification | Contact established |
| Discovery | Active conversation | Needs analysis, pain identification | Pain confirmed, budget estimated |
| Qualification | MEDDIC/BANT validated | Champion identified, timeline set | Criteria met, next step agreed |
| Demo / Evaluation | Solution presented | POC, technical validation | Positive feedback, eval criteria clear |
| Proposal | Commercial discussion | Pricing, legal, security review | Verbal agreement, negotiation in progress |
| Closed Won | Contract signed | Countersigned order form | Revenue recognized |
| Closed Lost | Deal not won | Loss analysis recorded | Loss reason documented |

#### Pipeline Coverage
- **Definition:** Total pipeline value ÷ revenue target for the period
- **Benchmark:** 3x–5x pipeline coverage is standard; enterprise deals may require 5x–7x
- **Formula:** If Q1 target = $1M and pipeline = $3.5M → 3.5x coverage
- **Warning signals:** Coverage below 3x with < 4 weeks in quarter = high miss risk
- **Action:** If coverage drops below 3x, trigger pipeline acceleration plays immediately

#### Deal Velocity Formula
```
Deal Velocity = (Number of Opportunities × Win Rate × Average Deal Size) ÷ Sales Cycle Length (days)
```
Improve deal velocity by: increasing win rate, raising ACV, shortening cycle, or adding opportunities.

### 4. Sales Qualification Frameworks

#### MEDDIC / MEDDPICC (Enterprise B2B Gold Standard)
| Letter | Element | Questions to Answer |
|---|---|---|
| M | Metrics | What measurable business outcome does the customer want? |
| E | Economic Buyer | Who controls the budget? Have we met them? |
| D | Decision Criteria | What criteria will they use to choose? Do we win on those criteria? |
| D | Decision Process | What are the steps to get a contract signed? |
| P | Paper Process | Legal, security, procurement — who is involved and how long does it take? |
| I | Identify Pain | What specific pain is driving urgency? What happens if they don't solve it? |
| C | Champion | Is there someone inside who will advocate for us when we're not in the room? |
| C | Competition | Who else is being evaluated? What is their preferred alternative? |

**When to use MEDDPICC:** Any deal with ACV > $25K or multi-stakeholder buying committee.

#### BANT (Early-Stage / Mid-Market Qualification)
- **Budget:** Do they have budget allocated or can they secure it?
- **Authority:** Is this person the decision-maker or do we need to reach higher?
- **Need:** Is there a confirmed, urgent need that our product solves?
- **Timeline:** When do they plan to make a decision and go live?

#### SPIN Selling Discovery Framework (Neil Rackham)
Use during discovery to help buyers articulate their own pain and value:
- **S**ituation: Understand the current state ("How are you handling X today?")
- **P**roblem: Uncover the pain ("What challenges does that create?")
- **I**mplication: Amplify the cost of inaction ("What does that mean for the business?")
- **N**eed-payoff: Let them articulate the value ("What would it be worth to solve this?")

#### The Challenger Sale (Dixon & Adamson)
The Challenger seller: **teaches** something new, **tailors** the message to stakeholder priorities, and **takes control** of the sale.
- **Teach:** Reframe how the prospect thinks about their problem; use data and insights they don't have
- **Tailor:** Adapt the message to the economic buyer vs. technical buyer vs. champion
- **Take control:** Drive next steps, create urgency, don't accept "we'll think about it"
- **Best for:** Complex enterprise deals where buyers are complacent or underestimating the problem

### 5. Predictable Revenue (Aaron Ross) Framework

The Predictable Revenue model separates the revenue engine into distinct, specialized roles:

| Role | Function | Metric |
|---|---|---|
| Inbound Lead Qualification (ILQ) | Respond to and qualify inbound leads | MQL → SQL conversion rate |
| Outbound SDR (Cold Prospecting) | Build pipeline from cold accounts via targeted outreach | SQLs sourced per month |
| Account Executive (AE) | Run discovery, demo, negotiation, and close | Win rate, quota attainment |
| Customer Success (CSM) | Onboard, retain, and expand existing customers | NRR, GRR, expansion MRR |

**Key principle:** Never ask your closers to prospect, and never ask your prospectors to close. Specialization drives predictability.

**Outbound motion (Spear Phishing approach):**
1. Identify 10–25 dream accounts per rep
2. Research each account deeply (pain, triggers, stakeholders)
3. Send hyper-personalized, short (3–5 sentence) outreach emails
4. Follow up with value, not pressure; offer insight, not a pitch

### 6. Funnel Optimization

#### The Revenue Funnel (B2B SaaS)
```
Awareness → Interest → Consideration → Intent → Evaluation → Purchase → Onboarding → Retention → Expansion
```

#### Key Conversion Benchmarks (B2B SaaS Industry Averages)
| Funnel Stage | Benchmark Conversion Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Visitor → Lead | 1.4%–2.9% | Higher for inbound/SEO traffic |
| Lead → MQL | 25%–41% | Varies by lead source quality |
| MQL → SQL | 13%–21% | Alignment-dependent; shared definitions matter |
| SQL → Opportunity | 42%–62% | Depends on qualification rigor |
| Opportunity → Closed Won | 20%–39% | Enterprise lower, SMB/PLG higher |
| Inbound lead response < 1 hour | 53% conversion | vs. 17% at 24+ hours — speed is critical |

**High-leverage optimization points:**
1. **MQL → SQL handoff:** Fastest ROI; shared definitions + SLAs between Marketing and Sales
2. **Lead response time:** Automate immediate response; never let inbound cool off
3. **Demo-to-proposal conversion:** Tighten discovery, qualify harder before demo
4. **Proposal-to-close:** Identify and engage economic buyer; use champion to drive internal process

#### Funnel Metrics to Track Weekly
- Lead volume by source (inbound, outbound, partner, PLG)
- MQL → SQL conversion rate by source
- SQL → opportunity conversion rate
- Average sales cycle length (by segment: SMB, mid-market, enterprise)
- Win rate by competitive displacement, deal size, and segment
- Lost deal analysis: top 3 loss reasons each quarter

## Key Metrics & KPIs

### Revenue Metrics Dashboard

| Metric | Formula | Healthy Benchmark | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) | MRR × 12 | Growing MoM | Monthly |
| MRR | Sum of all monthly recurring revenue | 10–20% MoM growth (early stage) | Weekly |
| New ARR | Net new contracts signed | Tracks new business motion | Monthly |
| Expansion ARR | Upsells + seat additions + usage growth | Target: 20–30% of new ARR | Monthly |
| Churned ARR | Lost customer contracts | < 5% annual for SMB, < 2% for enterprise | Monthly |
| Net Revenue Retention (NRR) | (Start ARR + Expansion − Contraction − Churn) ÷ Start ARR | ≥ 110% (great), ≥ 120% (best-in-class) | Monthly |
| Gross Revenue Retention (GRR) | (Start ARR − Contraction − Churn) ÷ Start ARR | ≥ 85% SMB, ≥ 90% enterprise | Monthly |
| CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) | Total S&M spend ÷ new customers acquired | < LTV/3 | Monthly |
| LTV (Lifetime Value) | ARPU × Gross Margin % ÷ Churn Rate | ≥ 3× CAC | Quarterly |
| LTV:CAC Ratio | LTV ÷ CAC | ≥ 3:1 (healthy), ≥ 5:1 (excellent) | Quarterly |
| CAC Payback Period | CAC ÷ (ARPU × Gross Margin) | < 12 months (SMB), < 18 months (enterprise) | Quarterly |
| Pipeline Coverage | Total pipeline value ÷ period revenue target | 3x–5x | Weekly |
| Win Rate | Closed Won ÷ total closed deals | 20–30% overall; track by segment | Monthly |
| Average Contract Value (ACV) | ARR ÷ total customers | Segment separately: SMB/MM/Enterprise | Monthly |
| Sales Cycle Length | Days from opportunity created to close | 30–45 days (SMB), 60–180 days (enterprise) | Monthly |
| Lead Velocity Rate (LVR) | MoM % growth in qualified leads | Positive and accelerating | Monthly |
| Quota Attainment | Revenue closed ÷ quota | ≥ 70% of reps at quota = healthy team | Monthly |
| Magic Number | Net new ARR ÷ prior quarter S&M spend | ≥ 0.75 = efficient growth; ≥ 1.0 = very efficient | Quarterly |
| Burn Multiple | Net burn ÷ net new ARR | < 1.5 = efficient; < 1.0 = excellent | Monthly |

### NRR Decomposition (Revenue Waterfall)
```
Beginning ARR
+ New Business ARR
+ Expansion ARR (upsells, cross-sells, seat growth, usage overages)
- Contraction ARR (downgrades)
- Churned ARR (cancellations)
= Ending ARR
```
Track all five levers monthly. NRR > 100% means the business grows even without new customers — the most powerful signal in SaaS.

## Revenue Forecasting

### Forecasting Methodologies

#### Bottom-Up Forecasting (Most Accurate for Operators)
- Start from individual deals, rep capacity, and pipeline stages
- Roll up rep-level forecasts → team → segment → company
- Inputs: pipeline by stage, stage conversion rates, weighted value, rep commit vs. upside
- **Best for:** Operational precision; catching risk early; board accountability

#### Top-Down Forecasting (Market/Strategic Context)
- Start from market size and growth targets, then allocate to segments and reps
- Inputs: TAM, historical growth rate, rep headcount, new hire ramp time
- **Best for:** Strategic planning, budget allocation, new market sizing

#### Hybrid Approach (Best Practice)
- Use top-down to set targets and bottom-up to validate or challenge them
- When top-down and bottom-up diverge by > 15%, investigate the root cause
- "The forecast is defensible when the top-down narrative matches the bottom-up evidence"

### Forecast Commit Categories
| Category | Definition |
|---|---|
| Commit | Rep is highly confident will close this period (90%+ probability) |
| Most Likely | Rep believes will close (70–89% probability) |
| Upside / Best Case | Could close with effort or luck (40–69% probability) |
| Pipeline | Earlier-stage; not expected to close this period |

### Forecast Review Cadence
- **Weekly:** 30-minute forecast call per team; update commit/upside/pipeline buckets; review slippage
- **Monthly:** Full pipeline review; compare actuals to forecast; update rolling 90-day forecast
- **Quarterly:** Board-level forecast presentation; scenario analysis (bear/base/bull)

### Pipeline Health Signals
- **Coverage ratio < 3x with < 6 weeks in quarter:** Immediate pipeline acceleration needed
- **Average deal age > 1.5× average sales cycle:** Deals are stuck; diagnose and act
- **Win rate declining 3+ months:** Qualification problem or competitive shift
- **Expansion ARR flat or declining:** CS/land-and-expand motion broken

## Partner & Channel Strategy

### Partner Ecosystem Framework

The Partnerships Pyramid (PartnerStack model):

| Tier | Partner Type | Revenue Contribution | Investment Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | Strategic alliances (large SIs, GSIs) | High ACV, long sales cycle | Very high (dedicated partner manager) |
| Tier 2 | Resellers & VARs | Moderate ACV, moderate volume | Medium (enablement + margin) |
| Tier 3 | Referral partners | Lower ACV, lighter engagement | Low (spiff + portal access) |
| Tier 4 | Technology/ISV partners | Pipeline via integrations | Low-medium (co-marketing) |
| Tier 5 | Marketplace listings | Self-serve pipeline | Low (listing + maintenance) |

### Building a Channel Partner Program

**Phase 1 — Foundation (Months 1–3):**
1. Define partner ICP: what types of partners serve your ICP?
2. Build partner agreement template (margin structure, referral fees, exclusivity)
3. Create partner portal (deal registration, enablement materials, support SLA)
4. Recruit 3–5 early "design partners" from your network
5. Set partner KPIs: referred pipeline, conversion rate, partner-sourced ARR

**Phase 2 — Enablement (Months 3–6):**
1. Build partner certification/training program
2. Develop co-marketing playbooks (joint webinars, case studies, event activations)
3. Define deal registration process and rules of engagement (CRO overlap protection)
4. Align compensation: partner spiffs, co-sell incentives, marketing development funds (MDF)

**Phase 3 — Scale (Months 6–12+):**
1. Segment partners by tier (revenue contribution, engagement, strategic fit)
2. Invest in top performers (dedicated partner manager, co-sell support)
3. Launch marketplace presence (AWS Marketplace, Salesforce AppExchange, HubSpot Ecosystem)
4. Build an annual partner summit or community

**Partner Economics:**
- Referral/affiliate commission: 10–20% of first-year ACV
- Reseller margin: 20–40% of list price depending on volume and services attached
- Technology partner co-sell: reciprocal referrals + shared customer success

**Industry Benchmarks:**
- Atlassian: 40%+ of revenue via channel partners
- Autodesk: ~65% of sales through 1,500+ indirect channels
- Target for mature SaaS: 20–30% of ARR from partner-sourced or partner-influenced revenue

## Customer Success & Expansion Revenue

### CRO Integration with Customer Success

Modern CROs treat CS as a core revenue function, not just a post-sales support team:
- **CS owns:** Onboarding, product adoption, renewals (GRR), expansion (upsell, cross-sell)
- **CRO accountability:** NRR, expansion ARR, churn prevention, CS-sourced pipeline

### Customer Lifecycle Revenue Framework

| Stage | Goal | CRO Metrics | CS Activities |
|---|---|---|---|
| Onboarding | Time-to-value (TTV) | Time to first value, activation rate | Kickoff call, success plan, training |
| Adoption | Product engagement | DAU/WAU, feature adoption %, health score | QBRs, usage reviews, proactive outreach |
| Renewal | GRR, renewal rate | Days-to-renewal, renewal rate by segment | Renewal playbook, EBR (Executive Business Review) |
| Expansion | Expansion ARR, NRR | Upsell conversion rate, expansion MRR | Usage-triggered upsell, seat expansion plays |
| Advocacy | Referrals, case studies | NPS, referral rate, G2/Capterra reviews | Reference program, community champions |

### Expansion Revenue Playbooks

**Usage-Based Upsell:**
- Trigger: Customer reaches 80% of plan limit
- Play: CSM alerts AE → automated email sequence → proactive upgrade conversation
- Goal: Prevent churn-by-overuse AND capture expansion revenue

**Seat Expansion:**
- Trigger: Power users sharing logins or requesting additional access
- Play: Track new user invitations within account → outreach to expand seats
- Goal: Grow ARPA (average revenue per account) organically

**Cross-Sell:**
- Trigger: Customer successfully using Module A; Module B solves adjacent pain
- Play: CSM identifies fit → AE runs cross-sell conversation
- Goal: Increase product attachment rate, raise switching costs

**NRR Improvement Framework (McKinsey):**
Companies with sophisticated value realization and adoption journeys produce NRR ~7 percentage points higher than peers. Prioritize:
1. Customer health scoring (product usage, support tickets, NPS, payment history)
2. Proactive churn playbooks (triggered at early warning signs, not at renewal)
3. EBR (Executive Business Review) cadence: quarterly for enterprise, biannually for mid-market
4. Success plan co-creation: define measurable outcomes with the customer at onboarding

### NRR Benchmarks
| NRR | Assessment |
|---|---|
| < 80% | Severe churn problem; company is shrinking |
| 80–100% | Below average; net contraction from existing base |
| 100–110% | Solid; holding its own with modest expansion |
| 110–120% | Great; meaningful expansion offsetting churn |
| > 120% | Best-in-class; company can grow without new customers |

## Building & Managing the Sales Team

### Sales Team Structure by Stage

| Company Stage | ARR | Sales Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-revenue / Seed | $0–$500K | Founder-led sales; 0–1 AE |
| Early traction | $500K–$2M | 2–4 AEs, 1–2 SDRs, CRO or VP Sales |
| Growth | $2M–$10M | 5–10 AEs, 3–5 SDRs, Sales Manager, CS team |
| Scale | $10M–$50M | Segmented: SMB, Mid-Market, Enterprise; RevOps function |
| Late stage | $50M+ | Global teams, field sales, partner sales, overlay specialists |

### Hiring for Sales

**First AE profile (early-stage startup):**
- Has sold B2B SaaS at a startup (not only big-company selling)
- Comfortable with ambiguity and building process, not just following it
- Can do their own prospecting and discovery (no SDR to hand them leads)
- References: talk to 3+ people they've sold with or managed

**SDR profile:**
- Hungry, coachable, high activity tolerance
- Strong writing (most SDR work is email/LinkedIn)
- Metrics: 8–12 meetings booked per month is solid at early stage

**Compensation structure:**
| Role | Base | Variable (OTE target) | Accelerators |
|---|---|---|---|
| SDR | $50K–$65K | $20K–$30K (1× meetings booked) | 1.5× above 120% quota |
| Mid-Market AE | $80K–$100K | $80K–$100K (1:1 base:variable) | 1.5–2× above 100% quota |
| Enterprise AE | $100K–$140K | $100K–$140K | 2× for deals > 150% quota |
| VP/CRO | $150K–$200K | 30–50% variable tied to ARR | Equity: 0.5–2% depending on stage |

### Sales Playbook Components

Every sales team needs a documented playbook covering:
1. **ICP & persona cards:** Decision-maker, champion, and influencer profiles
2. **Discovery script:** SPIN/MEDDIC-based questions by persona
3. **Demo flow:** Standard 30-min demo outline with customization points
4. **Objection handling:** Top 10 objections with responses (price, competitor, timing, "we'll build it")
5. **Email sequences:** Outbound prospecting, inbound follow-up, re-engagement
6. **Proposal / pricing guide:** Discounting authority levels, multi-year incentives
7. **Competitive battlecards:** For each main competitor: strengths, weaknesses, landmines to plant
8. **Handoff SLAs:** Sales → CS handoff checklist and timing

### Sales Coaching & Performance Management

- **Weekly 1:1 with each AE/SDR:** Pipeline review, deal coaching, blockers
- **Monthly performance review:** Quota attainment, activity metrics, win/loss trends
- **Quarterly calibration:** Stack-rank the team; identify top performers (invest more), mid (coach), low (PIP or reassign)
- **Call recording review:** Use tools like Gong or Chorus; listen to 2–3 calls per rep per month
- **Win/Loss analysis:** Interview every Closed Lost; document loss reason; share patterns with product and marketing

## Cross-Functional Alignment

### The Revenue Operating Model

The CRO's north star is "one plan, one forecast, one source of truth" across:

| Function | CRO Partnership | Shared Metric |
|---|---|---|
| Marketing | Pipeline creation; MQL definitions and volume targets | MQL → SQL conversion, pipeline sourced by marketing |
| Product | Feature gaps blocking deals; PLG funnel conversion; pricing input | Product-qualified leads (PQLs), trial conversion rate |
| Customer Success | Expansion ARR; renewal rate; churn risk escalation | NRR, GRR, expansion ARR, time-to-value |
| Finance | Revenue forecast vs. plan; headcount hiring pacing; quota setting | ARR, revenue recognition, burn per $ of new ARR |
| Engineering | Security reviews blocking deals; integrations needed to close; SLAs | Deal acceleration (enterprise POC timelines) |

### SLA Framework (Marketing ↔ Sales)
Define and enforce:
- **Lead response SLA:** Marketing → Sales SDR response within 5 minutes for high-intent leads, 1 hour for all others
- **MQL definition:** Explicitly agreed scoring model; reviewed quarterly
- **SQL definition:** BANT or MEDDIC criteria met; both teams sign off
- **Pipeline contribution target:** Marketing-sourced % of total pipeline (target: 40–60% inbound)
- **Feedback loop:** Sales reports MQL quality weekly; Marketing uses data to refine targeting

### The Revenue Review Meeting Structure
- **Weekly pipeline review (Sales + CRO):** 45–60 min; stage-by-stage review of all active deals > threshold ACV
- **Monthly GTM sync (Marketing + Sales + CS):** 60–90 min; funnel metrics, pipeline review, ICP alignment, campaign feedback
- **Quarterly business review (QBR):** Cross-functional; full performance vs. plan; next quarter strategy; headcount requests

## Revenue Forecasting: Operational Playbook

### Weekly Forecast Call Agenda (30–45 min)
1. **Commit review (10 min):** AE by AE: what's in commit this period? Any changes since last week?
2. **Upside review (5 min):** What could pull in to close early?
3. **At-risk deals (10 min):** Any deals that slipped out or at risk of slipping?
4. **Pipeline gap analysis (5 min):** If commit < target, what pipeline needs to accelerate?
5. **Actions (5 min):** Specific deal-level actions assigned to reps

### Rolling 13-Week Pipeline View
Maintain a rolling 13-week view of pipeline by close date. Every week, assess:
- New pipeline created (by source: inbound, outbound, partner)
- Pipeline advancing (stage progression)
- Pipeline lost or slipped
- Closed Won

This cadence catches slippage early and prevents end-of-quarter surprises.

### Forecast Accuracy Goal
- Target: forecast within ±10% of actuals by Week 2 of the quarter
- Measure: track forecast submitted vs. actuals delivered for each rep and manager
- Reward accuracy: make forecast accuracy a performance metric, not just quota attainment
